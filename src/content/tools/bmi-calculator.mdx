## Understanding Your BMI Results

Your **Body Mass Index (BMI)** is one of the most widely used health metrics worldwide. Developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s, BMI provides a quick way to assess whether your weight falls within a healthy range relative to your height.

### BMI Categories

| BMI Range | Category |
|-----------|----------|
| Below 16 | Severe Underweight |
| 16 – 18.4 | Underweight |
| 18.5 – 24.9 | **Normal Weight** |
| 25.0 – 29.9 | Overweight |
| 30.0 – 34.9 | Obese (Class I) |
| 35.0 – 39.9 | Obese (Class II) |
| 40+ | Obese (Class III) |

### Why BMI Matters

Research published in *The Lancet* shows that BMI is strongly associated with overall mortality risk. Maintaining a healthy BMI reduces the risk of:

- **Heart disease** — the leading cause of death worldwide
- **Type 2 diabetes** — risk increases significantly above BMI 25
- **Joint problems** — excess weight puts pressure on knees and hips
- **Sleep apnea** — common in those with BMI above 30

### Limitations of BMI

While BMI is useful, it's not perfect:

1. **Athletes and muscular individuals** may have a high BMI despite low body fat
2. **Older adults** may have a "normal" BMI but higher body fat due to muscle loss
3. **Children and teens** should use age-specific BMI percentile charts
4. **Pregnant women** should not use BMI as a health indicator

For a more complete picture, consider also calculating your **waist-to-hip ratio** and **body fat percentage**.

### Tips for a Healthy BMI

- Aim for 150 minutes of moderate exercise per week
- Eat a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
- Monitor portion sizes — even healthy foods have calories
- Stay hydrated — sometimes thirst is mistaken for hunger
- Get 7-9 hours of sleep — poor sleep is linked to weight gain
